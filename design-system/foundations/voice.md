# Voice

The rules of how X-Ray Tech sounds — in product UI, marketing pages, emails, decks, and ads.

## The seven voice rules

1. **Be specific over generic.** Not "your certifications." → "your 18 of 24 CE points."
2. **Speak in second-person singular.** "You" and "your," never "users" or "professionals."
3. **Short sentences.** If a sentence has two commas and an "and," cut it.
4. **Name their world.** Use ARRT, IEMA, CE credits, Category A/B, modality, scope, expiry, renewal cycle. Not "compliance items," not "training events."
5. **No AI / SaaS jargon.** Banned: *AI-powered, platform, solution, intelligent, automated, seamless, robust, ecosystem, supercharge, transform, leverage, synergy, holistic, end-to-end.*
6. **No hype.** Banned: *revolutionary, game-changing, 10x, cutting-edge, unleash, next-generation, world-class, best-in-class.*
7. **Every claim has a number or a name.** Not "save time" → "renew in 4 fewer days." Not "trusted by hospitals" → "used by techs at 23 imaging centers" (only when true; if not yet true, just don't claim it).

## The 3-second feeling test

A technologist who has never heard of X-Ray Tech reads three lines of copy. After five seconds they should feel: *this was written for someone in my job, by someone who knows the difference between Category A and Category B credits.* If they feel "this is a generic compliance tool," the copy fails.

## Tone by context

| Context                  | Register                                                                              |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Homepage hero            | Direct, confident, specific. One sentence.                                            |
| Homepage subcopy         | One sentence that names ARRT/IEMA or "license points" explicitly.                     |
| Section eyebrow          | Two or three words, uppercase. Identifies the topic, not "introducing…"               |
| Feature card             | Title is 4–6 words, an action or noun. Body is one sentence, 18 words max.            |
| Product UI label         | Verb-noun. "Add certification." "Mark complete." Not "Click here to…"                 |
| Product UI empty state   | One sentence stating what would appear here + one action.                             |
| Toast / confirmation     | Past-tense fact. "Certification added." Not "🎉 Great job!"                            |
| Error message            | What broke + what to try. No "Oops." No "Something went wrong."                       |
| Email subject line       | Under 40 chars. The number or the name. "18 / 24 CE points logged."                   |
| Email body               | One-to-one note voice. "Heads up — your IEMA renewal is 90 days out."                  |
| Pricing                  | The number, the unit, what's in. No "starting at." No comparison tables that lie.     |
| Case study               | Quote first. Then the number. Then the name and place.                                |
| About page               | Why this exists, in one paragraph, named after Michelle.                              |
| 404 / empty              | One line acknowledging the missing thing + a link back.                               |
| Hiring post              | Two paragraphs. The role, the actual day-to-day, who you'd work with.                 |

## Grammar & punctuation house style

- **Sentence case** for all headlines, buttons, and nav. Not Title Case. Not ALL CAPS (except overlines ≤3 words).
- **Oxford comma**: yes.
- **Em-dashes**: with spaces — like this. Not endashes for em-dash purposes.
- **Numerals over spelled-out** for any number ≥ 2. "24 CE points." Not "twenty-four."
- **Acronyms**: ARRT, IEMA, CE — no periods. On first occurrence in a marketing surface, spell out once: "ARRT (American Registry of Radiologic Technologists)."
- **Dates**: `Mar 14, 2026` or `2026-03-14` in dense tables. Never `03/14/26`.
- **No emoji** in product UI, marketing, or transactional email. Period.
- **No exclamation marks** outside of a celebration-of-completion screen — and even there, only one.

## What to do when there's nothing specific to say

Stop and find the number. If you're tempted to write "powerful tools to manage your credentials," the right move is to walk away from the keyboard and find the actual fact. Examples:

- "We track 4 types of CE credits across ARRT and 12 state license programs."
- "Add a certification in 30 seconds — upload the PDF, we OCR the rest."
- "See your team's renewal calendar 365 days out."

If you cannot find a number, write nothing. Empty space is on-brand. Vague copy is not.
